    Other Interests

    • Beekeeping

      I started beekeeping in the Spring of 2020. Actually, that's not quite accurate. My girlfriend decided I was going to start beekeeping in the Spring of 2020. We had watched a documentary on declining bee populations and she decided to sign us up for some beekeeping classes and order a couple of hives.

      Fast forward to the day we got our bees...she got stung and decided beekeeping wasn't for her. Apparently, she was imagining a beekeeper's life to be like a real life Honey Nut Cheerios commercial, where bees greet her every morning by cheerfully pooping honey onto her breakfast.
      Spoiler Alert: That's not how it works.

      Beekeeping is not easy. In fact, it's often frustrating. And you do get stung. But it's totally worth it. Sure you can get honey from them once a hive is established (not a trivial feat btw), but bees are incredibly interesting in their own rite.

      Did you know that bees communicate with one another by dancing? Or that beehives operate pretty much exactly like airports?

      Or that any bee you come across is most likely going to be female?

      Or that bee swarms aren't necessarilly as dangerous as you might think?

      Or that bees are a vital part of the ecosystem and having them in your area has several benefits to your community?

      It's all true, and I'm going to try to write down some of the things I've seen and learned about bees. In the meantime, here are some useful resources I found when I was getting started:

      • The American Beekeeping Federation
      • Pollinator.org
      • The Pollinator Network at Cornell University
      • r/Beekeeping on Reddit
      • The University of Guelph Honey Bee Research Centre YouTube page
